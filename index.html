Global Energy Market

Global Gas Demand Trends

  • Following a surge in 2024, global natural gas demand growth slowed sharply in the first half of 2025; the International Energy Agency forecasts growth of roughly 1.3% for 2025 compared with 2.8% in 2024 (approximate values based on IEA quarterly reports).
  • Asia‑Pacific remains the primary driver of demand growth as economies rebound and expand LNG import capacity.
  • Europe’s demand recovery is muted due to energy efficiency measures and continued fuel switching away from gas.
  • IEA projections suggest demand growth could reaccelerate in 2026 as new LNG supply comes on line and economic headwinds ease.

International Competition

U.S. producers face competition from Australia, Qatar and new mega‑projects in Africa. The global market remains tight, with Asia absorbing most incremental supply while Europe competes aggressively to replace Russian pipeline gas.

Geopolitical & Pipeline Developments

Russia–China Pipeline Agreement

  • On 2 September 2025, Russia and China signed a legally binding memorandum to build the Power of Siberia 2 pipeline through Mongolia, potentially delivering 50 billion cubic metres (bcm) of gas annually (~4.8 Bcf/d)【11909390558688†L195-L203】.
  • The existing Power of Siberia pipeline’s capacity will rise from 38 bcm to 44 bcm, boosting current flows【11909390558688†L216-L219】.
  • Pricing, construction timelines and contractors remain unresolved; the agreement signals deepening energy ties between Moscow and Beijing【11909390558688†L210-L233】.
  • The project highlights China’s effort to diversify away from U.S. LNG while Russia seeks to replace lost European sales【11909390558688†L230-L267】.

Implications for U.S. LNG

The Power of Siberia 2 could displace some Asian LNG demand. However, long development timelines and ongoing negotiations mean U.S. LNG exports will remain critical for Asian buyers in the near term.

Environmental & Sustainability

Climate Impacts

  • Natural gas emits less CO2 than coal and oil, but methane leakage remains a concern; regulators are tightening emissions standards and monitoring requirements.
  • Renewable capacity additions continue to accelerate despite policy setbacks; solar and wind together are expected to supply the largest share of incremental U.S. generation in 2025–26【237861724867901†L392-L399】.
  • High gas prices in global markets encourage switching to coal in some regions, highlighting the need for price stability and environmental policies.
  • Energy efficiency and electrification initiatives aim to reduce end‑use consumption, tempering long‑term growth in gas demand.

Sustainability Strategy

Balancing energy security with decarbonization goals requires investment in leak detection, methane mitigation, and low‑carbon fuel alternatives. Stakeholders should consider the full life‑cycle emissions of energy portfolios.

Conclusion & Recommendations

Summary

This report highlights the dynamic nature of the 2025 energy landscape. Record natural gas production and robust export commitments have positioned the United States at the forefront of global energy supply. Yet uncertainties around weather, policy and global competition underscore the importance of agile strategies. Markets remain tight but well supplied, with production gains offset by full storage and regulatory headwinds.

Actionable Recommendations

  • Monitor weather signals closely, especially ENSO‑neutral indicators like the NAO/AO, to fine‑tune procurement.
  • Engage in long‑term hedging across 2026–2029 to capture backwardation and mitigate volatility.
  • Diversify supply portfolios with both U.S. and international LNG sources to manage geopolitical risk.
  • Advocate for streamlined permitting to accelerate infrastructure projects and renewable integration.
  • Invest in low‑carbon technologies (CCUS, hydrogen, battery storage) to future‑proof operations against policy shifts.
  • Strengthen engagement with regulators and communities to navigate evolving policy landscapes and ensure project acceptance.
